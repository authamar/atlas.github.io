RNOR Zero-Tax Strategy—Does It Hold Up?

A structured fact-check of the claim that capital gains from US equities can be tax-exempt in both the US and India during the RNOR window.

📋 What the RNOR Strategy Actually Claims

The claim has two parts, each resting on different legal foundations:

Part 1 — India

RNOR Exempts Foreign Income

This part is well-founded. Under the Indian Income Tax Act, Section 6, a Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident (RNOR) is not taxed on income that accrues or arises outside India. Capital gains from selling US ETFs after returning to India, while in RNOR status, generally fall into this category—meaning India would not tax them. RNOR status typically lasts 2–3 financial years after return, provided you qualify.

Part 2 — United States

NRA Portfolio Capital Gains Exemption

This part is real but conditional. Under US domestic law (IRC §871), a Non-Resident Alien (NRA) is generally not subject to US tax on capital gains from the sale of publicly traded stocks and ETFs. The India–US DTAA, Article 13, reinforces this by allocating capital gains taxing rights to the country of residence—which would be India during RNOR. Since India then exempts those gains under RNOR, the result is potentially zero tax in either country.

✅The strategy is not wrong—but the conditions that must be met are stricter than the headline suggests.

⚠️ Where the Claim Oversimplifies

1 The 183-Day Trap (US Side)

If you are physically present in the US for 183 or more days in the same calendar year you sell the securities, and your gains exceed losses, those gains can become taxable in the US at 30% under the NRA rules. The IRS Substantial Presence Test governs how these days are counted. If you leave mid-year and sell from India later in the same calendar year, count your US days for the entire calendar year carefully.

2 Visa Type Is Everything

The NRA exemption works cleanly for H-1B, L-1, F-1, and other non-immigrant visa holders—once you have properly terminated US tax residency by departing. However:

  • If you hold a US green card, you remain a “resident alien” for US tax purposes even after physically leaving.
  • Simply moving abroad does not end US tax residency for green card holders.
  • You must formally abandon the card (IRS Form 8854 + USCIS Form I-407) before the NRA exemption applies.
🔴Green card holders: formal abandonment is required. Moving abroad alone does not make you an NRA.

3 The DTAA Savings Clause

Article 1(4) of the India–US tax treaty has a “savings clause” that preserves the US’s right to tax its own citizens and residents regardless of what the treaty allocates. This does not override US taxation if you are still technically a US resident (e.g., via green card).

4 Exit Tax — If You Held a Green Card 8+ Years

Section 877A of the US Tax Code imposes a “deemed-sale” exit tax on “covered expatriates”—those who held a green card for 8 or more years before relinquishing it. If this applies, you face a tax on unrealised gains across your entire asset base on the day you exit. Note: this does NOT apply to H-1B visa holders, who are not lawful permanent residents.

⚠️This is a serious liability that a CPA must assess before departure, not after—but only for green card holders.

5 Dividends Are Different from Capital Gains

The NRA exemption covers capital gains on sales—it does not cover dividend income. While you hold US ETFs as an NRA, distributions will still have US withholding applied:

  • Standard rate: 30%
  • Reduced treaty rate under India–US DTAA: typically 15–25%, depending on classification
  • File a W-8BEN with your broker to claim treaty benefits proactively—many investors miss this

📄 W-9 vs W-8BEN — Which Form Do You Need?

These two forms are the IRS mechanism for telling a payer whether you are a US person (W-9) or a foreign person (W-8BEN). Using the wrong one is a false certification signed under perjury, and triggers incorrect withholding and reporting. Here is how they differ:

Form W-9 Form W-8BEN
Full name Request for Taxpayer Identification Number and Certification Certificate of Foreign Status of Beneficial Owner for US Tax Withholding
Who uses it US citizens and resident aliens (including H-1B holders currently in the US) Nonresident aliens only—foreign nationals living outside the US who are not US tax residents
Filed with The payer / requester (bank, broker, employer)—never sent to the IRS directly The withholding agent (bank, broker)—never sent to the IRS directly
Income reported on Form 1099 (interest, dividends, miscellaneous income) Form 1042-S (foreign person’s US source income)
Withholding if not provided 24% backup withholding under IRC §3406 30% NRA withholding under IRC §1441
Can claim treaty benefits? No—W-9 certifies US person status; treaty benefits for NRAs are irrelevant Yes—Part II allows claiming reduced withholding rate under a tax treaty (e.g., India-US DTAA)
Validity period Indefinite unless information changes 3 calendar years from date signed (e.g., signed Oct 2024 → expires Dec 31, 2027)
Key identifier required US Social Security Number (SSN) or EIN Foreign TIN (e.g., Indian PAN) and/or US ITIN if claiming treaty benefits

What happens if you use the wrong form?

🚫 Resident alien submits W-8BEN: This is a false declaration under penalties of perjury. The payer issues Form 1042-S instead of 1099, misclassifying your income. The IRS sees a mismatch when you file as a resident alien. Overwithholding and audit flags result. The payer may also face penalties.
⚠️ Nonresident alien submits W-9: The payer skips 30% NRA withholding, leading to under-withholding. The IRS can assess the full 30% that should have been withheld, plus interest and penalties, against the withholding agent. Your income is also reported incorrectly on 1099 instead of 1042-S.

The transition: when to switch from W-9 to W-8BEN

Once you permanently depart the US and your residency termination date is established, you must notify each withholding agent (bank, broker) within 30 days of that change in circumstances. Replace your W-9 on file with a Form W-8BEN—from that point forward, your accounts should issue 1042-S (not 1099) and apply NRA withholding rules. See Publication 515 for the full withholding agent rules.

💡 H-1B holders still living in the US: You are currently a US resident alien. You must use W-9—not W-8BEN—regardless of your intent to leave. Submitting W-8BEN before your departure date is incorrect and constitutes a false certification.

👥 H-1B Holder: Your Tax Status Before You Leave

If you are on an H-1B visa, still living and working in the US, and have been present for more than 31 days in this calendar year—here is exactly where you stand and what you do and do not qualify for.

1 Your Current Status: US Resident Alien

H-1B holders are subject to the Substantial Presence Test (SPT) and—unlike F-1/J-1 students—have no exempt-individual exception. Every day of physical presence counts. The SPT requires meeting both conditions:

Prong 1: ≥31 days present in the current calendar year  ✓ (you meet this)
Prong 2: (Days this year ×1) + (Days year−1 ×⅓) + (Days year−2 ×⅙) ≥ 183

For a typical H-1B holder with 2+ years of US presence, this formula easily exceeds 183—making you a resident alien taxed on worldwide income, just like a US citizen. You cannot claim the NRA capital gains exemption, and you must use Form W-9 with your financial institutions.

🚫 The closer connection exception to the SPT is also unavailable to you right now—it requires your tax home to be in a foreign country for the entire year. While you are still working in the US, your tax home is here.

3 Your Departure Year Will Be a Dual-Status Year

The year you leave the US will be a dual-status year—you are taxed as a resident alien from January 1 through your departure date, and as a nonresident alien from your departure date through December 31.

PeriodTax treatmentKey rules
Jan 1 → Departure date (resident period) Worldwide income taxed Graduated rates; all income from all sources; FICA applies; no standard deduction for the full year
Departure date → Dec 31 (NRA period) US-source income only 30% on FDAP (dividends etc.); capital gains from securities generally exempt if <183 NRA days in the year; treaty rates available with W-8BEN

Dual-status filing restrictions: You cannot file Married Filing Jointly, cannot claim the standard deduction, and cannot claim the Earned Income Credit. File Form 1040-NR marked “Dual-Status Return” with a Form 1040 statement attached for the resident period.

4 Your Residency Termination Date

For H-1B holders who met the SPT, the residency termination date is your last day of physical presence in the US for the calendar year (with up to 10 de minimis days of later visits allowed). To use an earlier termination date, you must:

  • No longer be a US resident after departure
  • Have your tax home in a foreign country for the rest of the calendar year
  • Have a closer connection to that foreign country than to the US for the rest of the year
  • Timely file a signed Residency Termination Statement with your tax return

✅ Your Action Plan — Step by Step

This is a consolidated, sequenced checklist for an H-1B visa holder planning to permanently return to their home country and execute the RNOR strategy. Steps are organised by timing.

🕒 Right Now — While Still in the US
  • 1
    Confirm your SPT status and count days carefully Use the Substantial Presence Test formula to verify you are a resident alien. Count every day of US presence in the current year and the prior two years using the weighted formula. This determines your current tax obligations and helps you plan the optimal departure date.
  • 2
    Continue using Form W-9 with all financial institutions Do not submit W-8BEN yet. You are a resident alien and must certify US person status on W-9. Submitting W-8BEN while still a US tax resident is a false certification under penalties of perjury.
  • 3
    Engage a US-India cross-border CPA now The departure year (dual-status return) is one of the most complex returns you will ever file. Engage a CPA with US-India international tax experience before you leave—not after. They will help you plan the optimal departure date, prepare Form 1040-C or 2063, and structure the dual-status return. See IRS Publication 519 for the full framework.
  • 4
    Plan your departure date strategically Your residency termination date equals your last day in the US. The earlier in the calendar year you leave, the shorter your resident period (and thus worldwide income exposure). However, also count your total US days against the NRA 183-day capital-gains test for the departure year—if you have already spent close to 183 days in the US, capital gains realised after departure in the same year may still be taxable.
  • 5
    File all outstanding prior-year US tax returns The IRS sailing permit process requires confirmation that all prior-year returns are filed and taxes paid. Ensure your returns for all years of H-1B residence are complete and any balances settled.
📅 Before Departure — At Least 2 Weeks Out
  • 6
    Obtain your IRS Sailing Permit (Departure Clearance) Most departing aliens must obtain an IRS departure clearance before leaving the US. Visit an IRS office (not more than 30 days before departure, allow at least 2 weeks). File either:
    • Form 2063 — simpler option if your tax affairs are straightforward and prior returns are filed
    • Form 1040-C — full tax return for the period January 1 through departure; required if the IRS determines 1040-C is necessary for tax collection
    See IRS Departing Alien Clearance and Topic 858 for full details.
  • 7
    Address state tax residency Notify your state tax authority of your change in residency. Some states (particularly California) aggressively assert continuing tax jurisdiction after physical departure if domicile ties remain. Document your departure: close local accounts, surrender your driver’s license, update voter registration, and keep records.
✈️ On and After Departure
  • 8
    Document your departure thoroughly Keep copies of your boarding pass, passport entry/exit stamps, I-94 departure record, and any records of establishing your new foreign tax home (employment contract, lease, utility bills, bank accounts in your home country).
  • 9
    Notify all withholding agents within 30 days Contact each US bank, brokerage, and employer within 30 days of your departure date. Inform them of your change in circumstances (you are now an NRA) and replace your W-9 on file with a Form W-8BEN. If claiming India-US treaty benefits to reduce dividend withholding, complete Part II of the W-8BEN with the treaty article and reduced rate. Accounts must switch to 1042-S reporting.
  • 10
    Monitor your US day-count for the departure year After departing, track any return visits to the US for the remainder of the calendar year. The NRA capital gains exemption under IRC §871(a)(2) applies only if your total US days as an NRA in the calendar year are fewer than 183. This is a separate, simpler count from the SPT formula.
📄 Tax Filing — Departure Year Return
  • 11
    File your dual-status return File Form 1040-NR marked “Dual-Status Return” at the top. Attach a Form 1040 (labeled “Dual-Status Statement”) showing income earned during the resident period (Jan 1 → departure date). Deadline: April 15 if you received wages subject to withholding; June 15 otherwise.
  • 12
    Attach the Residency Termination Statement File a statement signed under penalties of perjury establishing your residency termination date. It must include: your name, address, US TIN, visa type and number, passport number, tax year, last day of US presence, and facts establishing your tax home was in a foreign country after departure. See Residency Starting and Ending Dates.
  • 13
    Confirm: Form 8854 is NOT required for H-1B holders The expatriation tax (§877A) and Form 8854 apply only to US citizens and long-term lawful permanent residents (green card holders with 8+ years of LPR status). H-1B visa holders are not lawful permanent residents and are entirely exempt from these rules.
🇮🇳 In India — Activating the RNOR Window
  • 14
    Confirm RNOR eligibility with a CA in India before you land RNOR status requires that you were an NRI for 9 of the prior 10 Indian financial years, or present in India for 729 days or fewer in the prior 7 years. Engage an Indian Chartered Accountant before returning to confirm eligibility and plan your return timing. Returning in February/March (near the end of the Indian financial year) can maximise the number of RNOR years available to you.
  • 15
    Realise US capital gains during the RNOR window Once RNOR, capital gains from selling US stocks/ETFs are exempt from Indian tax (foreign income exemption under Section 6 of the Indian Income Tax Act). Combined with the NRA capital gains exemption on the US side, this is the zero-tax window the strategy targets. RNOR typically lasts 2–3 Indian financial years—use this window intentionally.
  • 16
    Manage dividends separately—they are always withheld Even during RNOR, US-source dividends from ETFs face US withholding (15–30%). Ensure your W-8BEN has a valid India-US treaty claim in Part II to get the reduced rate. Track withholding credits for potential refund or credit in India under the DTAA if needed.

✅ RNOR Qualification — Quick Checklist

You qualify for RNOR status in India if either condition is met for the relevant financial year:

  • You were a Non-Resident of India (NRI) for 9 out of the 10 financial years preceding the year of return
  • You were present in India for 729 days or fewer during the 7 financial years preceding the year of return
💡Most people who have lived abroad for several years satisfy one or both conditions. Confirm with a Chartered Accountant in India before assuming eligibility.

🎯 Bottom Line

The “zero tax in both countries” outcome is achievable—but it is a potential best case, not a guaranteed outcome. It works cleanly for H-1B and other non-immigrant visa holders who properly terminate US tax residency and document the departure. It does not automatically work for green card holders. The 183-day rule, dividend withholding, and the dual-status filing year are genuine complexities that require professional guidance.

Before relying on this strategy: confirm your SPT status, obtain an IRS sailing permit, file your dual-status return carefully, update W-9 → W-8BEN at all institutions after departure, and open the RNOR conversation with a CA in India well before you land.
